## LAND PILLAR

*200 responses from 50 residents*

## KEY ACTIONABLE INSIGHTS FOR COMMON GOOD ALLIANCE:

**Immediate Priorities:**
1. **Land trust has strong community buy-in** - Move quickly to capitalize on 78% support
2. **Focus on affordable homeownership first** - This is the clearest, strongest consensus
3. **Address grocery store gap** - Practical need with broad support across demographics
4. **Demonstrate quick wins** - Community needs to see action, not more planning

**Critical Success Factors:**
- Genuine resident control in governance (not just "community input")
- Transparency about property ownership and decision-making
- Protection for both homeowners AND renters
- Speed of implementation matters to aging population
- Learn from Cincinnati examples (what worked in Madisonville/Walnut Hills, what failed in OTR)

**Risk Factors to Address:**
- Deep skepticism based on decades of broken promises
- Fear of displacement is explicit and evidence-based
- Current city responsiveness is extremely low - build trust through action
- Fire department capacity concerns could derail development efforts

This analysis is based on systematic review of all 200 responses across the 50 community members and three neighborhoods.

## CAPITAL PILLAR

*200 responses from 50 residents*

# Community Voice Analysis: North College Hill Capital Investment Survey

## 1. TOP THEMES

**Access to Capital & Lending Barriers** (~85% of responses)
- Overwhelming focus on discriminatory lending practices, credit score requirements, and collateral demands that exclude residents from traditional financing

**Community Investment & Collective Economics** (~75% of responses)
- Strong desire for pooled community funds, mutual aid models, and keeping money circulating within the local Black economy

**Small Business Support & Entrepreneurship** (~70% of responses)
- Consistent calls for business incubators, technical assistance, mentorship, and lower-barrier pathways to business ownership

**Youth Development & Future Generation** (~45% of responses)
- Investment in education, job training, after-school programs, and teaching financial literacy/entrepreneurship early

**Systemic Discrimination & Historical Context** (~40% of responses)
- Recognition of redlining's legacy, references to historic Black economic success (West College Hill 77% homeownership), and ongoing racial disparities in lending

## 2. CONSENSUS POINTS

- **Near-universal support** (95%+) for a community investment fund specifically supporting Black-owned businesses
- **Shared frustration** with traditional banking systems that are seen as designed to exclude rather than include
- **Agreement** that collective action and pooled resources are necessary since external capital isn't flowing to the community
- **Recognition** that credit scores and debt-to-income ratios are structural barriers that punish past hardship
- **Desire** for transparency, accountability, and community control over any investment mechanisms
- **Understanding** that economic development must be multi-generational and intentional, not quick fixes

## 3. KEY CONCERNS

**Lending Discrimination Persists**
- Different interest rates for same bank in different neighborhoods
- Racial disparities in loan approval despite similar qualifications
- "Redlining never really ended, it just got digitized"

**Catch-22 Situations**
- Need car to get to job, can't get car loan without job
- Need credit history to build credit, can't get credit without history
- Need collateral for loans, can't build collateral without loans

**Predatory Financial Services**
- Payday lenders and high-interest options are only accessible alternatives
- "Predatory lenders target our neighborhoods... charging 400% interest"

**Time & Complexity Barriers**
- Working multiple jobs leaves no time for complicated applications
- Approval processes take so long opportunities pass
- "By the time you hear back, it's too late"

**Permanent Exclusion**
- Past bankruptcy, criminal records, medical debt create permanent barriers
- "One bad period and you're marked"
- COVID credit damage still affecting approvals

**Survey Fatigue & Skepticism**
- "A lot of surveys like this don't lead to anything. Hope this one's different"
- Desire for action, not just more questions

## 4. SUGGESTIONS

**Immediate Actions**
- Create community investment fund with transparent governance
- Offer microloans ($500-$5,000) with flexible requirements
- Launch financial literacy workshops integrated with access programs
- Establish matched savings programs to help build assets
- Create grant writing support to access existing funding sources

**Business Support Infrastructure**
- Business incubator/accelerator with shared workspace and low overhead
- Mentorship connecting established businesses (1996 Eatery, Twisted Nails) with new entrepreneurs
- Marketing and technical assistance for digital presence
- Equipment lending library for tools/resources
- Food truck incubator as lower-barrier restaurant pathway

**Structural Changes**
- Partner with Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs)
- Explore community land trust model for permanent affordability
- Ban or limit predatory lenders in the area
- Bring bank branch back to neighborhood (currently a "financial desert")
- Simplify application processesâ€”"meet people where they are"

**Leverage Existing Assets**
- Partner with churches (trust + resources already exist)
- Connect with Cincinnati's broader Black business networks
- Link to Hamilton County and university resources
- Promote existing programs like $25,000 facade grants more widely
- Use Stepping Stone Event Center as economic development hub

**Inclusive Design**
- Accommodate non-traditional income (gig work, seasonal, tips, self-employment)
- Create senior-accessible programs (not just online)
- Include second-chance pathways for justice-involved residents
- Address childcare as economic infrastructure
- Support women-owned businesses facing "extra barriers"

## 6. SENTIMENT BREAKDOWN

**Frustrated but Not Defeated** (60%)

## HOUSING PILLAR

*200 responses from 50 residents*

I'll analyze these 200 housing responses from 50 North College Hill residents.

## 1. TOP THEMES

- **Maintenance/Repair Costs** (~40%): Aging infrastructure (roofs, furnaces, foundations, plumbing, wiring), costly repairs for homes built in same era, difficulty affording updates
- **Affordability Barriers to Homeownership** (~35%): Down payment challenges, rising rents eating savings, credit issues, student loan debt, competition from investors
- **Property Taxes & Insurance** (~25%): Rapidly increasing property taxes on fixed incomes, insurance rate spikes, coverage challenges for older homes
- **Vacant/Neglected Properties** (~20%): Investor-owned homes poorly maintained, vacant houses attracting crime, impact on neighborhood property values
- **Infrastructure Gaps** (~18%): Missing sidewalks (kids walking in streets), drainage/flooding issues, sewer line repairs as homeowner responsibility

## 2. CONSENSUS POINTS

- **Strong desire for homeownership** as wealth-building and stability, particularly among current renters
- **Generational/legacy housing is valuable** - multiple families mention 2nd-4th generation ownership
- **The community itself is worth investing in** - residents consistently mention good neighbors, pride, location advantages
- **Down payment is the #1 barrier** to homeownership for those who could afford monthly payments
- **Old housing stock requires coordinated support** - homes from 1940s-1960s all need similar repairs simultaneously

## 3. KEY CONCERNS

**For Current Owners:**
- Property tax increases forcing out long-time/senior residents on fixed incomes
- Cascading maintenance costs overwhelming single-income and senior homeowners
- Unaffordable major repairs (roofs: thousands, sewer lines: $8,000, windows: $15,000)
- Insurance companies dropping coverage or raising rates 30%+ annually

**For Renters:**
- Landlords who don't maintain properties but raise rent ($150/month increases with no improvements)
- Saving for down payment while rent increases eat into savings - "chasing a moving target"
- Investor buyers beating out resident buyers with cash offers
- Section 8 discrimination and limited landlord participation

**Universal:**
- Vacant properties becoming neighborhood safety issues and depressing home values
- Lack of sidewalks creating dangerous conditions for children walking to school
- Finding trustworthy, fairly-priced contractors (particularly concern for women/minority homeowners)
- Lead paint abatement costs for families with children

## 4. SUGGESTIONS

**Top Resident Recommendations:**

1. **Down Payment Assistance** (mentioned most frequently)
   - Down payment assistance programs
   - Matched savings programs
   - Grants for essential workers/teachers

2. **Address Vacant Properties**
   - Fix up or tear down vacant homes
   - Give residents first right of refusal before investors
   - Right of first refusal for long-term renters when landlords sell

3. **Support Current Homeowners**
   - Low-interest home repair loans
   - Emergency repair funds
   - Property tax relief/freezes for seniors
   - Lead abatement assistance
   - Help with city-mandated sidewalk repairs

4. **Education & Support**
   - First-time homebuyer classes
   - Post-purchase homeowner education
   - Credit counseling and repair programs
   - Mentorship pairing first-generation with experienced owners
   - Vetted contractor network

5. **Systemic Changes**
   - Community land trusts for long-term affordability
   - Rent-to-own programs
   - Second chance programs for people with foreclosure/criminal backgrounds
   - Employer-assisted housing partnerships
   - Rental property minimum standards enforcement
   - Bilingual resources for growing Latino population

## 5. NOTABLE QUOTES

> "Every time I save a little, the rent goes up and eats into it." - DeAndre Jackson (on the affordability trap)

> "My landlord barely fixes anything. I've had a leaky faucet for months, reported it three times. The rent keeps going up but nothing gets repaired." - Patricia Coleman (on rental conditions)

> "Everything in these houses was built around the same time so it all breaks down together." - Darnell Washington (on aging housing stock)

> "Too many houses getting bought up by out-of-town investors who just want rent money. They don't care about the community." - Patricia Coleman (on investor impact)

> "Building wealth through homeownership is how we change things for the next generation." - Keisha Robinson (on generational impact)

> "One big repair bill shouldn't mean losing your house. We need a safety net." - Cynthia Harris (on homeowner vulnerability)

## 6. SENTIMENT BREAKDOWN

**Overall Tone: Determined but Frustrated** (60% challenging circumstances, 40% hopeful/proud)

- **Pride in Community**: Strong attachment, multi-generational roots, good neighbors
- **Frustration**: Systems not working (predatory landlords, investor competition, bureaucratic barriers)
- **Anxiety**: Financial insecurity, aging infrastructure, displacement fears
- **Hope**: Belief in North College Hill's potential, desire to invest if given opportunity
- **Determination**: First-generation homeowners, single parents, people rebuilding after setbacks

**Notable**: Despite significant challenges, nearly all respondents express commitment to staying in North College Hill - the issue is access and sustainability, not desire.

## 7. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

North College Hill residents face a housing crisis driven by aging infrastructure overwhelming current owners with repair costs, while aspiring homeowners struggle with down payment barriers and investor competition. The community demonstrates strong multi-generational attachment and desire for homeownership as wealth-building, but needs coordinated support: down payment assistance for renters, repair loans/emergency funds for owners, vacant property intervention, and infrastructure investment (particularly sidewalks for child safety). With approximately 60% homeownership currently represented and universal desire to stay in the community, residents are requesting targeted programs rather than displacement - they want to invest in North College Hill if given fair access and sustainable support systems.
